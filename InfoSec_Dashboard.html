Visualization 2 — Attack Occurrence Trends (2015–2024)
Percentage of reported incidents by attack type per year | Sources: Verizon DBIR 2015–2024, IBM X-Force 2020–2024
Trend Analysis: Phishing surpassed general malware as the #1 attack category around 2018–2019 and continues to grow. Ransomware grew 5.7× from 2015 to its 2022 peak. Supply chain and cloud attacks both grew 8× in a decade — the fastest-growing categories. General malware declined proportionally as attackers shifted to more targeted, higher-return techniques.

Sources: Verizon DBIR 2015–2024, IBM X-Force Threat Intelligence Index 2020–2024, ENISA Threat Landscape Reports 2018–2023

Visualization 4 — Risk Score vs. Security Spending
Relationship between calculated risk and global defense spending per threat | Source: Gartner 2023, Cybersecurity Ventures 2022–2024
Insight: A positive correlation confirms markets generally respond to risk levels. Supply Chain Attacks command the highest spending ($46B), reflecting recognition of an emergent, hard-to-control threat. Ransomware ($20B) and APT ($6B) show spending commensurate with their risk. Vishing and Social Media Exploitation appear under-invested relative to their risk scores.

Sources: Gartner Security Forecast 2023, Cybersecurity Ventures 2022, Ponemon Institute 2022, Allied Market Research 2023

Visualization 5 — Attack Type Composition Across Years (Stacked Bar)
Year-by-year compositional breakdown of incident types | Sources: Verizon DBIR 2015–2024, ENISA 2018–2023
Insight: The dominant General Malware segment visibly shrinks each year as Phishing expands. The emergence of Ransomware and Supply Chain/Cloud segments from 2016 onward represents new dominant threat families. Defenses built for 2015-era mass malware are insufficient for 2024's threat composition.

Sources: Verizon DBIR 2015–2024, ENISA Threat Landscape 2018–2023, IBM X-Force 2020–2024, CrowdStrike 2021–2024

Visualization 6 — Threat-to-Defense Mechanism Heatmap
Effectiveness of 12 defense mechanisms against 25 threats (0=None · 1=Low · 2=Medium · 3=High) | Source: NIST CSF, CIS Controls v8, MITRE ATT&CK
Insight: No single control is universally effective. MFA is highly effective against credential-based attacks but provides zero protection against zero-day exploits or supply chain attacks. SIEM and Zero Trust are the two broadest controls. Supply Chain Attacks and Zero-Day Exploits have the sparsest coverage across all 12 defenses.

Sources: NIST Cybersecurity Framework v2, CIS Controls v8, MITRE ATT&CK Mitigations, OWASP ASVS
